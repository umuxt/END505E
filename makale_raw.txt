A B S T R A C T
This study presents a production scheduling for unrelated parallel machines with machine and job sequence-
dependent setup times. The system performance measures to minimize include makespan, total tardiness,
and number of tardy jobs. The aim of the study is to develop a solution methodology that can solve the
problem for the large scale. First, the problem is formulated as a mixed-integer linear programming model. The
augmented 𝜀-constraint method is applied to find Pareto solutions for small problem instances. The purpose is
to demonstrate that Pareto solutions, which balance the trade-offs among three measures of performance, can
be found for these instances. Dispatching rule-based heuristics is developed to solve large problem instances.
The heuristics feature three dispatching rules that are designed to handle the dependent setup time. In addition,
these rules are combined into six variants using a time-based rule-switching mechanism. The heuristics are
tested with 18 problem instances, containing 244 to 298 jobs, in two demand scenarios derived from the
monthly demand data from an industrial user. Under each demand scenario, a set of heuristics that provides
the best performance with respect to the three measures is identified. The heuristics include combinations
of the shortest completion time and due date-based rules. Finally, a multi-criteria decision-making analysis is
performed to determine the conditions specified by the weight given to each measure, with which one heuristic
is preferred over the others.
1. Introduction
Effective production planning is critical for manufacturing systems,
particularly when dealing with complex job sequencing and scheduling
tasks. This paper addresses these challenges for production systems in-
volving non-identical machines, known as unrelated parallel machines.
The problem is motivated by a real problem at the largest steel pipe
manufacturer in Thailand. This production system consists of multiple
machines that differ in their capabilities and production rates, adding
complexity to scheduling as they operate in parallel. In each period
(month), there are jobs, i.e., production orders, to be processed with
their own production quantity and due date. All jobs can be processed
at the beginning of each period with no job priority. This implies that
a job may have different processing times for different machines.
The complexity is further increased by machine setup times. Specif-
ically, the setup time of a job depends on the machine to which it
is assigned and its sequence. For instance, if two adjacent jobs are in
different families, the setup time is longer than if they are in the same
family but are different in dimension. That is, the setup time for a job
depends not only on the machine, but also on the preceding job on that

time.
In each period (i.e., production month), jobs representing orders
from customers are placed before the beginning of the period such
that production scheduling is performed during the previous period.
Consequently, the setup operation of the first scheduled job on each
machine is performed before the beginning of the period. In other
words, the setup time of the first job on each machine is set to zero
for each production month.
This problem is also commonly found in many manufacturing in-
dustries, where there are several product families to be made on the
same set of unrelated machines. In most cases, the primary objective
is to determine a job schedule for each machine such that the jobs
are completed as early as possible. This is usually measured by the
makespan, that is, the completion time of the last scheduled job.
Moreover, these jobs usually have different promised delivery dates.
To measure how well the due dates are met, the tardiness of each job
and number of tardy jobs are calculated. In practice, minimizing the
makespan, total tardiness, and number of tardy jobs are conflicting

ance these performance measures, such that production efficiency is
sustained without sacrificing customer satisfaction. Although the trade-

scheduling literature, no study has attempted to offer a solution that
strikes a balance among these measures for the problem under study.
This study makes three major contributions to literature.
• First, we develop a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP)
model for the unrelated parallel-machine scheduling problem
with machine- and sequence-dependent setup times. The model
is adapted from the study by Avalos-Rosales et al. [1] to include
the total tardiness and total number of tardy jobs in addition
to the original objective function of minimizing the makespan.
Moreover, we implement the augmented 𝜀-constraint (AUGME-
CON) method to find compromise solutions among the three
measures. The application of the proposed model is demonstrated
using small problem instances. This contribution fills a gap in
the literature, where existing models either simplify setup times
or focus on single-objective optimization for unrelated parallel
machines.
• Second, we design dynamic dispatching rule-based heuristics
(DDR) for large problem instances. Specifically, three widely used
single dispatching rules, including shortest processing time (SPT),
earliest due date (EDD), and longest processing time (LPT), are
modified to consider machine- and sequence-dependent setup
times. This modification results in three dispatching rules de-
signed for the sequence-dependent setup scheduling problems. In
addition, the three rules are combined into six dispatching rules
that feature rule switching times. The mechanism that allows the
change of dispatching rules during the scheduling process is very
effective not only in this study, but also in scheduling problems
in general due especially to its ease of implementation.
• Third, based on the set of effective rules, a thorough multi-criteria
decision-making analysis is performed to further identify the
conditions in which each rule is most effective. These conditions
are specified by the relative weights of importance that a decision-
maker assigns to the three performance measures. The approach
is believed to be the first of its kind that has been performed
for a multi-objective scheduling problem. The benefit of this
method can be explored in other scheduling problem settings with
multiple objectives.

2. Literature review
Production scheduling problems on parallel machines have been
explored in numerous studies. These problems can be categorized using
various criteria, such as parallel machine classification, setup time, sys-
tem measures of performance, and solution methods. Parallel machines
can be classified as identical, uniform, and unrelated. Identical parallel
machines imply that the processing time of a job is the same for every
machine, which has the same production rate [2–9]. Studies on uniform
machines with different processing times and production rates can be
found in Li et al. [10] and Zandi et al. [11]. Unrelated parallel machines
have different production rates and specific machine restrictions, which
means that each job can be processed by some machines [1,12–21].
Setup time is the time required to prepare resources to process
the next task [22]. It is classified as sequence-independent, sequence-
dependent, and machine-dependent. Sequence-independent setup time
means that the setup time is constant regardless of the job or ma-
chine [18,23,24]. The sequence-dependent setup time depends on the
preceding job on the machine [12,19,25–30]. In other words, sequence-
dependent setup times are determined by a pair of adjacent jobs on a
machine. The machine-dependent setup time depends on the machine
assigned to process a job [1,26,28,30,31].
Several criteria are used for measuring the performance of schedul-
ing solutions, including the makespan, total tardiness, and number
of tardy jobs [32,33]. Studies focusing on minimizing the makespan
have been conducted by Avalos-Rosales et al. [1], Arroyo et al. [20],
Soper [21], Ezugwu [31], Shchepin and Vakhania [34], and Lee et al.
[35]. Research on minimizing total tardiness includes works by Logen-
dran et al. [12] and Yin et al. [36]. Finally, Su et al. [18] minimize the
number of tardy jobs.
In addition to single-objective optimization, seminal contributions
that take different objectives into account have emerged over the
years in the field of unrelated parallel machine scheduling. Chayu and
Chang [25] investigate the minimization of total weighted flow time
and total weighted tardiness, dealing with job sequence- and machine-
dependent setup times. Lin et al. [13] expand this research by targeting
critical scheduling objectives, including makespan, total weighted com-
pletion time, and total weighted tardiness. Torabi et al. [26] explore
the minimization of total weighted flow time, total weighted tardiness,
and machine load variation. Nikabadi and Naderi [27] simultaneously
minimize makespan, number of tardy jobs, earliness and tardiness.
In a subsequent study, Wang and Alidaee [28] undertake the order
acceptance and scheduling on unrelated parallel machines, introducing
a multi-objective mixed-integer linear programming model that min-
imizes total workload and machine fixed costs. Farmand et al. [37]
develop a bi-objective model that integrates identical parallel machines
scheduling and supply chain management. The model aims at mini-
mizing both time-based and cost-based measures of performance. The
time-based measure consists of total weighted tardiness and operation
time, while the cost-based contains penalty for tardy orders, earliness,
and batch delivery cost. Lastly, Yepes-Borrero et al. [30] formulate a
bi-objective model that minimizes makepsan and maximum number of
resources needed.
For the methodology, a mixed-integer linear programming model
(MILP) can solve only small problem instances. Therefore, many re-
searchers have developed heuristics for solving large-scale problems.
Popular heuristics for unrelated parallel machines include genetic algo-
rithm [1,13,14,18], and simulated algorithm [17,18]. Other heuristics
found in literature reviews include branch and bound [19], iterated
greedy [20,30,38], polynomial-time approximation scheme [15], three-
phase [29], and composite dispatching rule [19]. Yin et al. [36] modify
the iterated greedy algorithm to their scheduling problem. In addition,
Chyu and Chang [25] introduce a Pareto evolutionary approach for
a two-objective (total weighted flow time and weighted tardiness)
scheduling problem, involving job sequence- and machine-dependent
setup times. Lin et al. [13] extend the work of Chyu and Chang [25]
by modifying the genetic algorithm to find multi-objective (includ-
ing makespan, weighted completion time, and weighted tardiness)
solutions in scheduling problems. Their algorithm shows exceptional
proficiency in generating non-dominated solutions. Torabi et al. [26]
propose a multi-objective particle swarm optimization (MOPSO) algo-
rithm. Nikabadi and Naderi [27] employ multi-objective genetic algo-
rithm (MOGA) and simulated annealing (SA). Wang and Alidaee [28]
develop a list-scheduling-based multi-objective parthenogenetic algo-
rithm (LS-MPGA). Their computational experiments demonstrate the
effectiveness of the LS-MPGA in achieving trade-off optimization ob-
jectives. Bektur and Sarac [29] propose two heuristics, simulated an-
nealing and Tabu Search, which use an initial solution from modified
apparent tardiness cost with setups (ATCS) dispatching rules. Far-
mand et al. [37] adopt meta-heuristic algorithms such as MOPSO and
non-dominated sorting genetic algorithm II (NSGA-II) for solving an in-
tegrated problem of identical parallel machines scheduling and supply

According to Table 1, no research has studied a scheduling problem
with multiple objective functions of makespan, total tardiness, and the
number of tardy jobs in production systems of unrelated parallel ma-
chines with machine- and sequence-dependent setup times. Motivated
by this research gap, this study aims to address the following aspects.
• The formulation of a multi-objective MILP model that can pro-
vide Pareto solutions, which represents the best trade-offs among
makespan, total tardiness, and number of tardy jobs.
• The capability and limit of the MILP model is examined through
a numerical experiment that is adapted from a practical problem.
• In addition to mathematical model, different dynamic dispatch-
ing rule-based heuristics are proposed, constructed, and exper-
imented. The purpose is to offer an alternative approach that
can find high-quality solutions within a reasonable time. This
aligns with the need of industries, especially those with unrelated
parallel machine systems, for a scheduling method that con-
sumes a minimal amount of time for generating good production
schedules.
• To the best of our knowledge, there is one previous study by
Bektur and Sarac [29] that includes sequence-dependent setup
time in the ATCS dispatching rule. Instead of constructing only
one dispatching rule, our work modifies three different rules that
are commonly employed in practice.
3. Model development
3.1. Problem statement
This study considers an unrelated parallel-machine scheduling prob-
lem with machine- and sequence-dependent setup times. The problem
descriptions are as follows.
– There are 𝑛jobs to be processed in each period (i.e., a month).
Each job consists of the required production quantity and the due
date.
– All jobs are released for production at the beginning of each
period, that is, all jobs have the same release time. There is no
priority among jobs in terms of their importance.
– The production system consists of 𝑚unrelated parallel machines.
– The processing time of a job on a machine depends on the
job-machine pair.
– Each machine can process a subset of all jobs. For the jobs that a
machine cannot process, their processing times are set to a large
value.
– Setup time is machine- and sequence-dependent, i.e., setup time
of a job is determined by a pair of preceding job and the job itself
on a given machine.

Constraints (2)–(11), (13)–(14), and (16)–(17)
Since multi-objective model involves optimizing several objective
functions simultaneously, achieving a single solution that is optimal
for all objectives that are in conflict is generally impossible. As a
result, a widely adopted approach for dealing with these problems
is the 𝜀-constraint method. This technique facilitates the exploration
of interactions among objectives such that a balanced compromise
among them can be achieved [41]. In this study, we utilize an aug-
mented version of the 𝜀-constraint (AUGMECON) method [42] to ad-
dress the proposed multi-objective model for production scheduling
problem. The AUGMECON method is designed to generate efficient
Pareto-optimal, non-dominated solutions in multi-objective problems,
with proven effectiveness in producing pareto optimal results [43].
For a detailed explanation of this method, audiences are referred to

5.2. Large problem instances
Large problem instances are created using 18 months of real pro-
duction data from January 2019 to June 2020. Each problem instance
is a month of production data. In total, 413 products are classified into
26 product families. These products are steel pipes with (1) different
thicknesses, which are categorized as being in the same family, that
is, requiring a short setup time; (2) different diameters, medium setup
times depending on the change in diameters; or (3) different shapes:
round, rectangular, or square, which are classified as being in different
families, that is, requiring a long setup time. The monthly demand
data contain customer orders ranging from 1359 to 3639 orders. Each
order contains up to 40 products. Customer orders requesting the same
products are aggregated into the same job, that is, production orders.
On average, the number of jobs in each month ranged from 244 to 298.
The production line has ten unrelated parallel machines with vary-
ing capabilities in terms of the product families that each machine
can process and the production rate. The setup times for products
in the same family but with different thicknesses ranged from 20 to
40 min. However, products in the same family with different diameters
or products in different families require setup times ranging from 3 to
11 h, depending on the sequence of jobs, that is, changing diameters
requires a shorter setup time than changing shape. Statistical summary
of the large instances is presented in Table 14.
Several months of data are tested to demonstrate the effectiveness of
the proposed heuristics in various demand scenarios. Naturally, there is
some variation in monthly demand and an increasing trend in demand
over time due to business growth (see Fig. 4). Specifically, the monthly
demand in 2019 is lower than that in 2020, except in October 2019.
Interestingly, there is growing demand despite the Covid-19 pandemic.
The problem instances are grouped using the k-mean clustering method
to characterize the demand scenarios. Based on the analysis, the 18
months of demand data can be divided into two clusters: low demand
and high demand. The number of clusters is chosen based on the
highest silhouette score [44], as shown in Table 15.
Each problem instance is tested with three single dispatching rules
and six combined rules in combination with six rule-switching times.
In a month, there are up to 25 working days, eight hours per shift,
two shifts per day, and up to five hours of overtime, which results
in a maximum of 525 production hours. Therefore, the rule-switching
times are set between 200 and 450 h with a step size of 50 h, which
is 200, 250, 300, 350, 400, and 450. In summary, the computational
experiment tests (3 + 6 × 6) = 39 rules on 18 problem instances and
three measures of performance, including makespan, tardiness, and
number of tardy jobs.

5.3. Selection of dispatching rule under multiple measures of performance
The effective dispatching rules (two single and three combined
rules) and their performances on the three measures are summarized
in Table 20. The selection of dispatching rules becomes a multi-criteria
decision-making problem. A closer look reveals that some rules, al-
though effective, are inferior to others. Specifically, under high de-
mand, the [SCT & SC-LPT: 450] is outperformed by SCT in all three
measures. Therefore, [SCT & SC-LPT: 450] is eliminated for further
analysis under high demand. As a result, four rules remain for the
high-demand scenario. Similarly, under low demand, SCT and [SCT &
SC-LPT: 450] are eliminated by [SCT & SC-EDD: 450] and SC-EDD by
[SC-EDD & SCT: 200]. In other words, there are only two rules, [SC-
EDD & SCT: 200] and [SCT & SC-EDD: 450], to be considered under
low demand.
For the multi-criteria decision-making problem, the weight of im-
portance assigned to each measure is critical to the rule selection
process. Although these weights are usually specified by a decision
maker (DM), we chose to perform rule selection analysis under exhaus-
tive enumerations of the three weights. The purpose is to obtain the
conditions specified by the relative weights assigned to the measures,
where each dispatching rule is the most effective. The weights for the
makespan, total tardiness, and number of tardy jobs are denoted by
𝑤𝐶, 𝑤𝑇, and 𝑤𝐿, respectively. Their values are varied from 0.01 to
0.98, under the condition that the weights add up 1, i.e., 𝑤𝐶, 𝑤𝑇, and
𝑤𝐿∈[0.01, 0.98] and 𝑤𝐶+𝑤𝑇+𝑤𝐿= 1. Because the three measures are
numerical, an appropriate selection method is the technique for order
preference by similarity to ideal solution (TOPSIS). The steps of TOPSIS
implemented in this study are described below [45].

5.4. Discussion
The findings of this research provide contributions to the produc-
tion scheduling research area, particularly in the context of unrelated
parallel machines with machine and job sequence-dependent setup
times. The results are in line with previous research that emphasizes
the complexity of scheduling problems in manufacturing environments.
The classification of parallel machines into identical, uniform, and

the challenges posed by machine and job sequence-dependent setups,
which have been less frequently explored.
Performance of the modified dispatching rules, i.e., SPT, EDD, and
LPT, demonstrates a clear benefit over traditional methods. Previous
studies have shown that these rules can yield effective scheduling
solutions under certain conditions [4,5]. Our research extends this
understanding by incorporating machine and job sequence-dependent
setup times, thereby enhancing the applicability of these rules in real-
world scenarios. Moreover, the statistical analysis conducted, including
ANOVA and Tukey’s test, confirms that the performance of the SCT
and SC-EDD rules is superior to that of the SC-LPT rule across various
performance measures and demand scenarios. This finding is consistent
with earlier work that suggests the importance of rule selection based
on specific operational contexts [6]. The ability to adapt dispatching
rules to the unique characteristics of each scheduling problem is crucial
for improving production efficiency.
The multi-criteria decision-making analysis further enriches these
findings by demonstrating how different performance measures can
be prioritized based on the decision-maker’s preference. This aspect
of the research resonates with the literature that supports a tailored
approach to scheduling, where the relative importance of makespan,
tardiness, and job completion times can vary significantly depend-
ing on the production environment [9]. This study highlights that
combined dispatching rules, such as [SCT & SC-LPT] and [SCT & SC-
EDD], offer flexible and robust strategies that results in solutions with
improved makespan and acceptable tardiness compared to single-rule
approaches.
5.5. Research implication
Implications of the research findings for practical applications in
production scheduling, particularly for industrial users are as follows.
Firstly, the development of modified dispatching rules considering
machine and job sequence-dependent setup times provides a valuable
framework for optimizing production processes. Industrial users can
implement these tailored rules to enhance their scheduling efficiency,
leading to reduced idle times and improved machine utilization. By
minimizing setup times and effectively managing job sequences, man-
ufacturers can achieve higher throughput and better meet production
targets.
Secondly, the application of multi-criteria decision-making analysis
underscores the necessity for decision-makers to consider multiple
performance measures when evaluating scheduling solutions. Indus-
trial users often face competing system measures, such as minimizing
makespan while reducing tardiness. This research enables a holistic
approach to scheduling, allowing for the balancing of various perfor-
mance metrics based on specific operational goals. Such flexibility can
lead to improved customer satisfaction through timely deliveries and
enhanced overall operational performance.
Thirdly, the findings emphasize the importance of adaptability in
scheduling systems. As industries increasingly face demand fluctuations
and changes in production requirements, the ability to adjust schedul-
ing strategies becomes crucial. The proposed heuristics and dispatching
rules can be easily modified to accommodate different production
demand scenarios, enabling manufacturers to respond swiftly to market
changes. This adaptability not only enhances operational resilience,
but also positions companies to capitalize on emerging opportunities.
Lastly, the research provides a foundation for further exploration of
hybrid scheduling approaches that combine the strengths of various
heuristics and optimization techniques. Industrial users can benefit
from integrating advanced scheduling algorithms into their existing
systems, leading to more sophisticated decision-making processes and
improved production planning.
6. Conclusion
This study involved an unrelated parallel-machine scheduling prob-
lem with sequence- and machine-dependent setup times. Three system
performance measures, including makespan, total tardiness, and the
number of tardy jobs, are considered. For small problem instances,
MILP models are formulated to find optimal solutions as well as Pareto
solutions through the implementation of the AUGMECON method. For
large problem instances, dispatching rule-based heuristics that consider
sequence- and machine-dependent setup times are developed. They
include SCT, SC-LPT, SC-EDD, and six combinations of these rules. For
the combined rules, the appropriate rule-switching time is determined
through computational experiments.
The heuristics are tested using 18 datasets of the monthly demand
from the industry. The results indicate that under the high-demand
scenario, the best dispatching rule for the makespan, total tardiness,
and number of tardy jobs are SCT, SC-EDD, and [SCT & SC-EDD: 450],
respectively. For the low-demand scenario, the best rule for makespan
is [SCT & SC-EDD: 450], whereas [SC-EDD & SCT: 200] is most effective
for both the total tardiness and the number of tardy jobs. In practice,
the decision makers may assign multiple relative weights of importance
to the performance measures, which determines the rule that should be
used. We perform a complete enumeration of the weights to determine
a series of conditions in which the DM prefers one rule over the others.
The result in this research is the stepping stone for several research
directions in the future. One of them is to develop new dispatching
rules or to extend the problem to multiple operations in sequence,
where there are unrelated parallel machines in each operation. In
addition, to further improve the performance, the dispatching rules can
be implemented as a part of a meta-heuristic algorithm that generates
solutions on the Pareto front for large problem instances.
